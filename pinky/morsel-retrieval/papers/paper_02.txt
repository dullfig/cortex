Characterizing Sleep Fragmentation in Residents of Assisted Living Facilities: An Actigraphy Study
Marcus Lindqvist, Yuna Park, Dmitri Harwell

Sleep fragmentation is a well-documented contributor to daytime fatigue, falls, and cognitive decline in older adults, yet its causes in the assisted living setting remain incompletely characterized. We instrumented 94 residents across three assisted living facilities with wrist-worn actigraphs for fourteen consecutive nights and paired the recordings with morning sleep diaries and a brief nocturnal event questionnaire. Mean total sleep time was 6.4 hours with a mean of 4.8 discrete wake episodes per night lasting longer than five minutes. Wake episodes clustered in the second half of the night, consistent with the well-known advance in circadian phase typical of this age group. When residents who reported a specific waking cause were asked to attribute their awakenings, bathroom visits dominated at 41% of attributed events, followed by ambient noise from corridor activity at 18%. Notably, nocturnal leg cramps accounted for 14% of attributed awakenings and were the single largest category of unexplained or involuntary wakings that did not involve a deliberate action by the resident, a figure substantially higher than nursing staff estimates collected in a parallel survey. Pain from chronic musculoskeletal conditions and vivid dreams each accounted for under 10%. Facility-level variance was modest after adjusting for resident age and comorbidity burden, suggesting that architectural and staffing differences between the three sites contributed less to sleep fragmentation than resident-intrinsic factors. We conclude that interventions targeting frequent but individually minor causes of awakening — particularly those currently underrecognized by staff — may yield meaningful improvements in consolidated sleep for this population, and that actigraphy combined with brief morning recall is a practical instrument for identifying such targets in routine care.
