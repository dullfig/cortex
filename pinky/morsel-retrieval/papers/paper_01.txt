Dietary Magnesium Intake Patterns in Community-Dwelling Adults Over 70: A Regional Nutrition Survey
Helena Vasquez, Peter Ahlgren, Ruth Okonkwo

We report results from a cross-sectional dietary survey of 1,284 community-dwelling adults aged 70 and older across four counties in the upper Midwest, conducted between March 2021 and November 2022. Participants completed three non-consecutive 24-hour dietary recalls administered by trained interviewers, supplemented by a food frequency questionnaire covering the preceding six months. Serum magnesium was measured in a subset of 412 participants who consented to phlebotomy. Mean dietary magnesium intake was 241 mg/day for women and 278 mg/day for men, both below the RDA of 320 and 420 mg/day respectively. Fifty-eight percent of participants fell below two-thirds of the RDA. Intake was positively associated with whole grain consumption (r=0.44, p<0.001) and leafy green vegetable frequency (r=0.31, p<0.001), and inversely associated with reliance on prepared frozen entrees. Serum levels correlated only weakly with dietary intake (r=0.18), consistent with prior work suggesting tight homeostatic regulation. In an exploratory analysis of self-reported symptom questionnaires collected alongside the dietary recalls, participants in the lowest intake quartile reported nocturnal leg cramps at roughly twice the rate of those in the highest quartile, a finding that reached nominal significance and warrants targeted follow-up. We did not observe significant associations between magnesium intake and self-reported cognitive complaints, fall history, or grip strength after adjustment for age, sex, and total caloric intake. Our results suggest that dietary magnesium inadequacy remains common in this population despite widespread availability of fortified foods, and that targeted dietary counseling emphasizing whole grains and leafy greens may be a low-cost intervention worth evaluating in a prospective trial. Limitations include the cross-sectional design, reliance on self-report for symptom data, and the regional nature of the sample, which may not generalize to populations with substantially different dietary traditions.
