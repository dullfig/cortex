Evaluating Scheduling Heuristics in Enterprise Endpoint Antivirus Deployments
Clara Denton, Joaquin Pereira, Marta Nystrom

Endpoint antivirus (AV) products ship with default scan schedules that attempt to balance detection coverage against performance impact on end users. We audited the default and deployed scan configurations of six widely used enterprise AV products across a corpus of 218 customer deployments collected via opt-in telemetry from a managed security service provider. Scheduled full-system scans were by far the dominant scheduled activity, accounting for 78% of scheduled scan time, with quick scans and signature updates making up the remainder. Default full-scan windows clustered heavily in the early morning hours of Tuesday and Wednesday local time, a pattern that appears to originate from a single vendor's default that was subsequently copied by competitors during the late 2000s. We instrumented a subset of customer endpoints with lightweight performance counters during scheduled scan windows and found that full scans produce sustained, bursty contention on the memory bus that is readily observable from unprivileged user-space processes running on the same host, with scan-window memory latency increases ranging from 14% to 61% depending on storage subsystem and working set. Scan durations varied widely, from 22 minutes on SSD-backed workstations to over four hours on older spinning-disk laptops. We propose a simple adaptive scheduler that jitters scan start times across a seven-day window and defers scanning when the host reports active user sessions; a trial deployment on 31 endpoints reduced user-visible slowdown complaints by 46% over eight weeks without measurably reducing scan coverage. We argue that the industry's convergence on a shared default scan window is a latent operational risk and recommend vendors randomize default schedules at install time.
