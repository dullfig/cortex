Graph Neural Networks for Malware Family Classification from Static Call-Graph Features
Linnea Forsberg, Hemant Krishnamurthy

Accurate malware family attribution supports triage, incident response, and threat intelligence correlation, but remains challenging under the adversarial pressure of obfuscation and packer variation. We evaluate a graph neural network (GNN) classifier operating on static call graphs extracted from unpacked Windows PE binaries against a curated corpus of 48,210 samples spanning 112 families. Nodes in each call graph correspond to functions, with features derived from opcode n-grams and imported API categories; edges correspond to direct call relationships. We compare a standard graph convolutional network, a graph attention network, and a simpler gradient-boosted baseline operating on flattened whole-binary features. The graph attention variant achieved the highest macro-F1 of 0.863, compared to 0.821 for the GCN and 0.774 for the gradient-boosted baseline, with the largest gains concentrated in families with fewer than 200 training samples where the attention mechanism appears to exploit shared subgraph motifs across related families. Ablation experiments suggest that approximately half of the attention model's advantage over the GCN can be attributed to its willingness to assign near-zero weight to packer-inserted call-graph noise. Inference latency on a single GPU averaged 14 ms per sample, adequate for inline use in a sandbox pipeline. We release our preprocessing scripts and a deduplicated evaluation split, and we discuss the limits of static call-graph features against samples employing heavy control-flow flattening, which we find degrade all three classifiers roughly equally.
