Fatigue Life of Recycled-Content 6061 Aluminum Bicycle Frames Under Accelerated Cyclic Loading
Tomas Reinholt, Aiko Sasaki, Benedikt Orr

Recycled-content aluminum alloys are increasingly attractive for mid-market bicycle frame production due to reduced embodied energy and material cost. We evaluated the fatigue performance of welded 6061-T6 frames containing 40% post-consumer recycled content against matched frames produced from virgin stock. Thirty-two frames (sixteen per group) were subjected to an accelerated cyclic loading protocol adapted from EN 14781, with a peak pedal load of 1,200 N applied at 4 Hz until either failure or the 200,000-cycle endurance limit was reached. Recycled-content frames exhibited a mean cycles-to-failure of 187,400 compared to 198,100 for virgin-stock frames, a modest but statistically significant reduction (p=0.04) attributable primarily to inclusion-initiated crack nucleation near heat-affected zones. Fractography confirmed that initiation sites in the recycled group were more frequently associated with sub-surface oxide stringers. In a secondary exposure sub-study designed to probe the combined effect of environmental chloride, six frames from each group were pre-conditioned for 90 days in a coastal test rack located 40 meters from the shoreline before undergoing the same loading protocol; pre-conditioned frames, regardless of recycled content, showed micro-crack propagation rates approximately 12% faster than their dry-stored counterparts, with the effect localized to weld toes on the down tube and chainstay. We conclude that recycled-content 6061 frames are suitable for general consumer use but that manufacturers should consider tighter inclusion-control specifications for frames intended for coastal markets, and that current frame warranty language, which assumes a largely benign service environment, may underestimate real-world fatigue exposure for a subset of customers.
