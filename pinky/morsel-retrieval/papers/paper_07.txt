A Randomized Placebo-Controlled Trial of Low-Dose Melatonin in Rotating-Shift Healthcare Workers
Priya Raghunathan, Oskar Lemaitre, Fenna de Wit

Rotating shift work is associated with chronic sleep disruption, gastrointestinal complaints, and elevated cardiometabolic risk. We conducted a twelve-week double-blind randomized placebo-controlled trial of 0.5 mg sublingual melatonin administered thirty minutes before the intended main sleep period in 142 rotating-shift nurses and allied health workers at a single tertiary hospital. The primary endpoint was self-reported sleep quality as measured by the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI). Secondary endpoints included actigraphy-derived total sleep time, sleep onset latency, and a brief daytime fatigue scale administered at the end of each shift. Participants in the melatonin arm showed a mean PSQI improvement of 1.8 points compared to 0.6 points in the placebo arm (p=0.009), with the largest gains seen among workers on night-to-day transitions. Actigraphy confirmed a modest reduction in sleep onset latency of approximately 9 minutes in the melatonin arm but no significant change in total sleep time. Daytime fatigue scores improved in both arms, with a non-significant trend favoring melatonin. Adverse events were rare and mild, most commonly morning grogginess reported by 7% of melatonin-arm participants. We conclude that low-dose melatonin is a safe and modestly effective adjunct for sleep quality in rotating-shift healthcare workers, though its effect on objective sleep duration appears smaller than its effect on subjective sleep quality, and larger multi-site trials are warranted before recommending routine occupational use.
