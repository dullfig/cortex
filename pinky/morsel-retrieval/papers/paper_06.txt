Atmospheric Dispersion of Chloride Aerosols from Winter Road De-icing Operations in a Mid-Sized North American City
Ingrid Halvorsen, Rafael Cienfuegos

Road salt application is the dominant winter de-icing strategy in most North American cities, and while the hydrological and soil-loading consequences of runoff are well studied, the airborne fraction generated by vehicle-induced resuspension has received comparatively little attention. We deployed a network of eleven passive chloride-deposition samplers and three active aerosol monitors along transects extending from 10 to 1,200 meters from instrumented arterial roadways in a mid-sized city over two winter seasons. Chloride deposition fluxes followed an approximately exponential decay with distance from the road but remained measurably above regional background values out to 800 meters during active de-icing periods, and brief spikes in airborne chloride were detectable at the farthest sampler within 30 minutes of heavy truck passage following fresh salt application. Aerosol size distributions indicated that a substantial fraction of resuspended chloride was carried on particles in the 1 to 5 micrometer range, consistent with extended atmospheric residence times and potential for intrusion into poorly sealed building envelopes and covered but unventilated storage areas. Deposition was strongly modulated by wind direction, with downwind receptors showing three- to fivefold higher fluxes than upwind controls at matched distance. We discuss implications for urban vegetation stress, building facade corrosion, and the design of municipal monitoring programs, and we note that the 800-meter scale of measurable chloride drift is substantially larger than the buffer distances assumed in most environmental impact assessments for new arterial construction.
