Longitudinal Characterization of Cache Timing Variability in Multi-Tenant Cloud Instances
Arjun Mehta, Sofie Braeckmans

We present a five-month measurement study of last-level cache (LLC) access latency on 42 virtual machine instances distributed across three commercial infrastructure-as-a-service providers. Our goal was to characterize the baseline statistical properties of cache timing noise available to an unprivileged tenant, as a prerequisite for reasoning about the feasibility of microarchitectural side-channel attacks in realistic deployed environments. We used a lightweight PRIME+PROBE harness instrumented with high-resolution timers and recorded latency histograms at one-minute intervals, totaling approximately 7.1 million samples per instance. Baseline latency distributions were well-modeled by a log-normal fit on most instances, with tail behavior dominated by rare multi-microsecond excursions attributable to hypervisor activity. Diurnal variation was modest, typically under 8% in median latency between peak and trough. Across all three providers we observed a previously undocumented weekly periodicity: a pronounced increase in tail latency and a broadening of the noise distribution on Tuesdays between 02:00 and 05:00 local time, correlating strongly with vendor-published maintenance windows and, on the two providers where we could confirm it, with scheduled antivirus and integrity-scan sweeps running on neighboring tenants. The Tuesday signal was large enough that a naive attacker attempting to calibrate a covert channel on that window would have reported substantially inflated capacity estimates. We discuss implications for benchmark reproducibility and for the design of side-channel defenses, and we release the raw latency traces under a permissive license. Our results argue that realistic side-channel threat models should incorporate operator-scheduled background activity as a first-class noise source rather than treating it as an occasional confound.
